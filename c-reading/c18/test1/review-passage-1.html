Questions 1–3

1. lettuces
Explanation: In the initial description: after mentioning strawberries, the passage continues: “From identical vertical tubes nearby burst row upon row of lettuces; near those are aromatic herbs…” → clearly, lettuces are grown in vertical tubes together with strawberries and herbs.

2. 1,000 kg
Explanation: The passage states: “20 staff will harvest up to 1,000 kg of perhaps 35 different varieties of fruit and vegetables, every day.” → the daily amount is 1,000 kg. (The answer can also be written as 1000 kg or 1,000 kg.)

3. consumption (or food consumption)
Explanation: Hardy says: “if enough unused space can be developed like this, there’s no reason why you shouldn’t eventually target maybe between 5% and 10% of consumption.” → the word required is consumption (meaning the city’s overall consumption). Writing “food consumption” is also acceptable (two words).

Questions 4–7

4. pesticides
Explanation: The passage states: “most of the fruit and vegetables we eat have been treated with something like 17 different pesticides.” → intensive farming uses many pesticides.

5. journeys
Explanation: The passage states: “they’ve travelled an average of 2,000 refrigerated kilometres … varieties are selected for their capacity to withstand such substantial journeys.” → therefore the answer is journeys.

6. producers
Explanation: The passage states: “80% of the price I pay goes to wholesalers and transport companies, not the producers.” → producers receive only a small portion of the income, so the answer is producers.

7. flavour (or flavor)
Explanation: Hardy says: “You can select crop varieties for their flavour, not their resistance to the transport and storage chain.” → the key advantage is selecting crops for their flavour. Note: both spellings flavour (British) and flavor (American) are usually accepted.

Questions 8–13

8. TRUE — Urban farming can take place above or below ground.
Explanation: The passage gives examples of farming on rooftops and also states “Strawberries are being grown in disused shipping containers, mushrooms in underground carparks.” → since farming occurs both above ground (rooftops) and below ground (underground), the answer is TRUE.

9. NOT GIVEN — Some of the equipment used in aeroponic farming can be made by hand.
Explanation: The passage only mentions that the equipment “weighs little, can be installed on almost any flat surface and is cheap to buy: roughly €100 to €150 per square metre.” — there is no information stating that it can be made by hand or self-built. Therefore, the answer is NOT GIVEN.

10. FALSE — Urban farming relies more on electricity than some other types of farming.
Explanation: The passage states the opposite: “It is cheap to run, too, consuming a tiny fraction of the electricity used by some techniques.” → aeroponic farming uses less electricity than some other techniques, so the statement claiming it uses more electricity is contradictory → FALSE.

11. TRUE — Fruit and vegetables grown on an aeroponic urban farm are cheaper than traditionally grown organic produce.
Explanation: The passage states: “Produce grown this way typically sells at prices that, while generally higher than those of classic intensive agriculture, are lower than soil-based organic growers.” → the price is lower than soil-grown organic produce, so the answer is TRUE.

12. FALSE — Most produce can be grown on an aeroponic urban farm at any time of the year.
Explanation: The passage mentions seasonal limitations: “There are limits … and much of the produce is suited to the summer months.” → most crops cannot be grown year-round → the statement is incorrect → FALSE.

13. NOT GIVEN — Beans take longer to grow on an urban farm than other vegetables.
Explanation: The passage only states: “And beans tend to take up a lot of space for not much return.” — this refers to space and efficiency, not growth time. Since there is no information about growth duration, the answer is NOT GIVEN.
